Comparison of e-book readers

An e-book reader is a portable electronic device that is designed primarily for the purpose of reading e-books and periodicals.

E-book readers are similar in form to a tablet computer, and usually refer to readers that use electronic paper. The main advantages of e-book readers are better readability of their screens especially in bright sunlight and longer battery life. This is achieved by using electronic paper technology to display content to readers. When disconnected from the web, an e-book reader's battery will last from weeks to months. A tablet computer typically has a faster screen capable of higher refresh rates which makes them more suitable for interaction.

Any device that can display text on a screen can act as an e-book reader, but without the advantages of the e-paper technology.

Different kinds of screens

All electronic paper types offer lower power consumption and much better sunlight contrast than LCDs. Some offer backlights in low-light reading situations. With the backlight turned off, all have a similar appearance to "ink on paper" and readable in bright environments.

  • eInk: although "eInk" is usually used casually to talk about all electronic paper displays. Technically, "eInk" was trademarked by the company Vizplex, which sells the majority of electronic paper displays. eInk is the name of their earliest model.
  • eInk pearl: Vizplex's 2nd generation model, which has higher contrast and a greater number of different levels of gray than their earlier models.
  • SiPix: an early competitor to Vizplex, which had almost instantaneous page turns. Vizplex bought a majority share in SiPiX in 2010, and has integrated their technology.
  • Triton Color E Ink: one of several competing color electronic paper displays on the market right now. This one is manufactured by Vizplex.
  • Mirasol color display: a competing color electronic paper technology sold by the company Mirasol
  • LG flex: Electronic paper that allows for a flexible screen. With these displays, you can bend the e-reader without damaging it and do things like fold it up to put in your pocket.

Other mobile text viewers

ImageLink Some portable multimedia players and smartphones include a text viewer, e.g. several Cowon players, including the Cowon D2 and the iAUDIO U3 and Mobipocket Reader for Symbian OS and Windows Mobile mobile phones and devices. Adobe Reader mobile also turns Windows Mobile devices (for example, Samsung Omnia) into e-book viewers. Apple's iPad, iPhone,<ref>...</ref> and iPod Touch are acquiring status as e-book readers through a variety of e-reader apps. WordPlayer, FBReader, Aldiko or Mantano Reader turn Android phones into e-book readers. The BlackBerry PlayBook has a number of excellent e-book applications. Palm OS based devices and smartphones are also usable for reading books. PalmOS supports PalmDoc, iSilo, Mobipocket reader, PDF, HTML conversion, text format, Handstory, TealDoc among many other software titles, and word processing.

Some mobile devices support word processing. Some fully functional tablet notebooks (with screens that turn 180 degrees and lie with the back to the keyboard) and subnotebooks are used as e-book readers.
